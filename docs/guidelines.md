# Scientific Research Methodology

> **Lesson 1 — Introduction to scientific research**

## What is Research?

**Definition** *(Merriam-Webster)*

1. Careful or diligent search.
2. Investigation or experimentation aimed at the **discovery** and interpretation of facts, **revision** of accepted theories or laws in the light of new facts, or **practical application** of such new or revised theories or laws.
3. The **collecting of information** about a particular subject.

### Who does research?

Most of us do (informal) research every day. In the broadest terms, we do research whenever we gather information to answer a question that solves a problem:

- *Problem:* To settle a bet, I need to know when Michael Jordan was born.
  *Research:* You Google "Michael Jordan birthday."
- *Problem:* I'm curious about a new species of fish.
  *Research:* You search the Internet for articles in newspapers and academic journals.

### Why do research?

Research by others determines most of what each of us believes about the world.

## Science & Technology

- **Science** (from Latin *scientia*, "knowledge") builds and organizes knowledge in the form of testable explanations and predictions about the universe (natural, social and artificial).
- **Technology** (from Greek *téchne*, "art, skill") is the sum of techniques, skills, methods, and processes used to produce goods or services, or to accomplish objectives.
- Both are based on the **scientific method**.

### The scientific method — DNA example

1. **Question** — How is genetic information stored in DNA?
2. **Hypothesis** — DNA has a helical structure.
3. **Prediction** — If DNA had a helical structure, its X-ray diffraction pattern would be X-shaped.
4. **Testing** — Photo 51 showed an X-shape.
5. **Analysis** — Detailed analysis of the diffraction pattern confirmed a helix.

## Scientific Research

**Definitions**

1. Creative, **systematic** activity undertaken to increase the stock of knowledge — including knowledge of man, culture and society — and the use of this knowledge to devise new applications *(OECD / UNESCO)*.
2. Investigation or experimentation aimed at the **discovery** and interpretation of facts, **revision** of accepted theories or laws, or **practical application** of new or revised theories or laws.
3. The **collecting of information** about a particular subject.

### Steps in conducting research

1. Identification of the research problem
2. Literature review (specifying the purpose of research)
3. Determining specific research questions
4. Specification of a conceptual framework (hypotheses)
5. Choice of a methodology (for data collection)
6. Data collection
7. Verifying data
8. Analyzing and interpreting the data
9. Reporting and evaluating research
10. Communicating findings and, possibly, recommendations

### Scientific research as a process

Research is a cycle rather than a straight line:

- Understanding the problem / asking the question
- Collecting information
- Analyzing information
- Drawing conclusions
- Generalization and comparison with other approaches
- New questions arising from the problem → and the cycle continues

The cycle is only complete when you **communicate** the results.

## Communicate: why write it up?

### Researchers write…

- **To remember what they find** — listing sources, assembling summaries, keeping lab notes.
- **To understand relationships among ideas** — arranging and rearranging results, building outlines and diagrams to see connections, contrasts, complications, and implications.
- **To gain perspective** — improving the thinking and seeing the ideas in a clearer light.

### Why turn it into a formal paper?

- It gives you new ways of thinking.
- Writing for others is more demanding, so your ideas — and the connections among them — become clearer.

### Conversations among researchers

- Research papers and term papers are **conversations**: you must be aware of your audience.
- What relationship do you want with your readers? You create a role for yourself **and** for your reader.

**Example.** The writers of the next two passages imagined different readers, with different questions and different levels of knowledge:

> **1a.** The control of cardiac irregularity by calcium blockers depends on calcium's activation of muscle groups through its interaction with the regulatory proteins actin, myosin, tropomyosin, and troponin in the sarcomere, the basic unit of muscle contraction.

> **1b.** Doctors can control irregular heartbeats with drugs called calcium blockers. When the heart contracts, its muscles are activated by calcium. The calcium interacts with four proteins that regulate contraction: actin, myosin, tropomyosin, and troponin. That interaction happens in the basic unit of muscle contraction, the sarcomere.

- **Situation 1.** The writer of (1a) casts herself and her readers as colleagues who already know how muscles work. The writer of (1b) casts himself as an expert patiently explaining a complex matter to readers who know little. If they judged their readers correctly, the readers will judge them favorably.
- **Situation 2.** If the passages were switched, a reader ignorant of muscle physiology would find (1a) indifferent to his needs, while an expert would find (1b) condescending. In either case the writers lose their readers by misjudging the relationship.

### Know your readers

- They have their own interests and preconceived ideas.
- Is your question a live issue for your community of readers?
- Where do your readers stand with respect to your answer?
- What do you expect of them — to accept new knowledge, change beliefs, or perform an action?

### Learning to work with problems

> "By now, all this talk about airy academic research may seem disconnected from a world in which so many people labor so hard at getting ahead or keeping others down. But in business and government, in law and medicine, in politics and international diplomacy, no skill is valued more highly than the ability to recognize a problem, then to articulate it in a way that convinces others both to care about it and to believe it can be solved, especially by you."
>
> — *The Craft of Research*

# From Topics to Questions

The process of choosing what to research narrows in four steps:

1. **Interests** — choose an interest in a broad subject area.
2. **Topics** — narrow the interest to a plausible topic.
3. **Questions** — question that topic from several points of view.
4. **Problems** — define a rationale for your project.

## 1. From an interest to a topic

- Start with what interests you most deeply.
- List four or five areas you would like to learn more about.
- Pick the one with the best potential for a topic that is specific and likely to lead to good sources of data.

**Tips — where to look for topics**

- Matters of interest in your field of study
- A recent textbook
- Another student, or your teacher / supervisor
- Another course
- A general bibliographical resource in the library

**Caution.** Make sure your topic is rich in literature. If you pick a topic and only later discover the sources are thin, you will have to start over.

## 2. From a broad topic to a focused one

A topic is probably **too broad** if you can state it in fewer than four or five words. Narrow it by adding words of a special kind — conflict, description, contribution, development:

| Too broad | Focused |
|---|---|
| Food safety | Impact of US government regulation on food safety |
| The history of commercial aviation | The contribution of the military to the development of the DC-3 in the early years of commercial aviation |

A focused topic makes it easier to recognise the gaps, inconsistencies, and puzzles you can question — which is what turns a topic into a research question.

**However…** don't narrow so far that you can't find data. And avoid the beginner's mistake:

> "Once they have a focused topic, many new researchers immediately start plowing through all the sources they can find, taking notes on everything they read."
>
> — *The Craft of Research*

## 3. From a focused topic to questions

If a writer asks no specific question worth asking, he can offer no specific answer worth supporting — and without an answer to support, he cannot select, from all the available data, just what is relevant.

Four perspectives help narrow a topic to a research question:

- Identify the **parts and whole** of your topic.
- Trace its **history and changes**.
- Identify its **categories and characteristics**.
- Determine its **value**.

## 4. From a question to its significance

Once you have a question that holds your interest, pose a tougher one about it: **So what?** Beyond your own curiosity, why would others think it worth asking? Use three steps:

1. **Name your topic** — *I am working on / studying …*
2. **Suggest a question** — *… because I want to find out how / why …*
3. **Motivate the question (find a rationale)** — *… in order to understand how / why …*

**a) Name your topic**

> I am studying the causes of the disappearance of large North American mammals…
>
> I am working on Lincoln's beliefs about predestination and their influence on his reasoning…

**b) Suggest a question** — add a *because I want to find out how / why / whether…* clause:

> …**because I want to find out whether they were hunted to extinction**.
>
> …**because I want to find out how his belief in destiny influenced his understanding of the causes of the Civil War**.

**c) Answer "So what?"** — add an element explaining why the question matters to others:

> …**in order to help my reader understand whether native peoples lived in harmony with nature or helped destroy it**.
>
> …**in order to help my reader understand how Lincoln's religious beliefs may have influenced his military decisions**.

# Writing a Research Paper — Summary

> **Lesson 4**

## Title

- The first thing readers read.
- A good title helps readers understand specifically what is to come: refine it, and include the key concepts the reader will then expect to find throughout the paper.

**Compare:**

- The Crusades
- Political motives and the Crusades
- The Crusades as a force in European unity: preventing internal political and theological division through external distraction

## Introduction: Context – Problem – Answer

> As part of its program of Continuous Quality Improvement ("CQI"), Motodyne Computers plans to redesign the user interface for its Unidyne™ online help system. The specifications call for self-explanatory icons that let users identify their function without verbal labels. Motodyne has three years' experience with its current icon set, but it has no data showing which icons are self-explanatory. Lacking such data, we cannot determine which icons to redesign. This report provides data for eleven icons, showing that five of them are not self-explanatory.

**Context (common ground)** — establish a shared understanding between reader and writer about the larger issue:

> "As part of its program of CQI, Motodyne Computers plans to redesign the user interface… Motodyne has three years' experience with its current icon set…"

**Problem** — disrupt the context with a *condition* of incomplete knowledge and its *consequences*:

> "…but it has no data showing which icons are self-explanatory. Lacking such data, we cannot determine which icons to redesign."

- A **direct cost:** "Lacking such data, we cannot determine which icons to redesign."
- A **benefit:** "With such data, we could determine which icons to redesign."

**Answer** — toward the end of the introduction, either state the gist of the solution explicitly or promise it by stating where the paper is headed:

> "This report provides data for eleven icons, showing that five of them are not self-explanatory."

# Research Arguments

> **Writer:** Who is my reader?
> **Reader:** Why should I believe what I read?

## Defending ideas — an everyday conversation

- **A:** I hear you had a rocky time last semester. *(A poses a problem, as a question.)*
- **B:** Better, I hope. *(B makes a claim that answers the question.)*
- **A:** Why is that? *(A asks for a reason to believe B's claim.)*
- **B:** I'll be taking elective courses. *(B offers a reason.)*
- **A:** Why do you think that'll make a difference? *(A doesn't see how the reason is relevant to the claim.)*
- **B:** When I take courses I'm interested in, I work harder. *(B offers a general principle relating reason to claim.)*
- **A:** What courses? *(A asks for evidence.)*
- **B:** History of architecture and introduction to design. *(B offers specific instances behind the reason.)*
- **A:** But what about that calculus course you have to take again? *(A contradicts B's reason.)*
- **B:** I know I had to drop it, but I found a really good tutor. *(B acknowledges the objection and responds.)*

## The elements of an argument

In a research work you:

- make a **claim**,
- back it with **reasons**,
- support them with **evidence**,
- **acknowledge** and **respond** to other views,
- and sometimes explain your **principles** of reasoning.

**Five questions to ask on your readers' behalf:**

1. What is my **claim**?
2. What **reasons** support my claim?
3. What **evidence** supports my reasons?
4. Do I **acknowledge** alternatives, complications, or objections — and how do I **respond**?
5. What **principle** (the **warrant**) makes my reasons relevant to my claim?

## Building the argument

A **claim** is the answer to your research question, supported by reasons and evidence.

- **Claim:** TV violence can have harmful psychological effects on children…
- **Reason 1:** …**because** their constant exposure to violent images makes them think violence is natural.
- **Reason 2** (supporting reason 1): Constant exposure also makes them unable to distinguish fantasy from reality.
- **Evidence:** Smith (1997) found that children ages 5–7 who watched more than three hours of violent television a day were 25% more likely to say that most of what they saw on television was "really happening."

> Base claims on reasons; base reasons on evidence.

## Acknowledge alternatives and respond

Anticipate as many of the readers' questions as you can, then acknowledge and respond to the most important. A critical reader may not accept your claim based solely on your views — they may know other claims, reasons, or evidence; interpret the evidence differently; or draw different conclusions.

For example, readers might wonder whether children are drawn to TV violence because they are *already* inclined to violence. Add an acknowledgment and response:

> …*(claim, reasons, and evidence as above)*… **Of course, some children who watch violent entertainment might already be attracted to violence** *(acknowledgment)*. **But Jones (1999) found that children with no predisposition to violence were as attracted to violent images as those with a violent history** *(response)*.

**Examples from a real review**

> The authors claim to have used "an exploratory descriptive research methodology" and cite J. Lazar's book. I checked the book: the closest phrasing (p. 29) refers to "exploratory descriptive investigations" in the context of interviews, observations and focus groups. The reader would expect a *confirmatory* study of the findings in section 2.3, but instead gets another exploratory one — yet section 2.3 actually reports a confirmatory study (an experiment). This looks like a misinterpretation of the term, so the paper calls for terminological harmonization across sections.

> It seems the intervention (the adaptations) does not make a significant difference. To confirm this, the authors could have computed inferential statistics as the review indicated, but chose not to. They claim in the rebuttal that the sample size is too small — despite the fact that inferential tests (including the t-test) work well with such sample sizes.

## Warranting the relevance of your reasons

Even when readers agree a reason is *true*, they may object that it isn't *relevant* to your claim. A **warrant** is the general principle that justifies relating a particular reason to a particular claim.

- **Claim:** Children exposed to large amounts of violent entertainment tend to become adults who think violence is a legitimate part of daily life,
- **Reason:** because as children they adopt the violent values in what they see.
- **Warrant / principle:** "When children adopt certain values, as adults they tend to accept as 'normal' any behavior that reflects those values."

The principle of reasoning goes beyond the specific topic of TV violence. If it is not clear how the reasons connect, add these connections explicitly.

## Final remarks on claims

- **Be specific.** "TV inflates estimates of crime rates" → "Reports of violence on local TV lead regular viewers to overestimate, by as much as 150%, both the crime rate in their neighbourhood and the personal danger to themselves and their families."
- **Acknowledge limiting conditions** readers might plausibly think of — e.g. small sample size — not trivial ones. *"It can be concluded that the epicenter was 50 miles south of Tokyo, assuming the instrumentation was well calibrated."*
- **Qualify your certainty.** We are rarely 100% certain a claim is unqualifiedly true:

> "We **wish to suggest** a structure for the salt of deoxyribose nucleic acid (DNA)… A structure has already been proposed by Pauling and Corey. **In our opinion**, this structure is unsatisfactory for two reasons: (1) **We believe** that the material which gives the X-ray diagrams is the salt, not the free acid; (2) **some** of the van der Waals distances appear to be too small."
>
> — Crick & Watson, *Nature*, 1953

## Final remarks on evidence

Distinguish weaker from stronger evidence:

> **Claim:** American higher education should review its "hands-off" policy toward off-campus drinking,
> **Reason:** because dangerous binge drinking has become common.
> **Weaker evidence:** "The injuries and deaths it causes have increased in frequency and intensity, not only at big 'party' schools but among first-year students at small colleges."
> **Better evidence:** "In 2001–2006, there was a 19% increase in episodes of binge drinking resulting in death or injury among first-year students at a representative sample of small colleges (fewer than 2,000 students; see Appendix 1)."

## Final remarks on acknowledgments and responses

Respond to the reader's predictable questions and objections.

**Question your problem:**

- Why do you think there's a problem at all? What are its costs or consequences?
- Why have you defined the problem as you have — is it conceptual or pragmatic?

**Question your solution:**

- What kind of solution do you propose?
- Have you stated your claim too strongly (exceptions, limitations)?
- Why is your answer better than others?

**Questions about the evidence** — the reader may say it is not the right kind (wants hard numbers, not anecdotes), not accurate, not current, not representative, or simply insufficient.

**Don't acknowledge too much** — too many objections distract from the core of your argument. Choose:

- plausible weaknesses you can rebut,
- alternative lines of argument important in your field,
- alternative evidence readers know,
- important counterexamples you must explain away.

**Select a few to respond to:**

> Some have argued that food can be addictive *(acknowledgment of alternative)*, but we are concerned here only with substances for which addiction is the norm *(reason why the alternative is irrelevant)*.

If that is not enough, add evidence and a subreason:

> …Some who taste chocolate once may be unable to resist it thereafter, but their number is a fraction of those addicted to crack cocaine after a single exposure *(subreason)*. Chernowitz (1998) found that just one exposure resulted in… *(evidence)*.

**Introducing responses** — useful signposts:

- [**Despite / Regardless of / Notwithstanding**] Congress's claims that it wants to cut taxes *(acknowledgment)*, the public believes that… *(response)*.
- [**Although / While / Even though**] there are economic problems in Hong Kong *(acknowledgment)*, Southeast Asia remains strong… *(response)*.
- This proposal [**may have / plausibly has**] some merit *(acknowledgment)*, but we… *(response)*.
- It is easy to [**think / imagine / claim / argue**] that taxes should… *(acknowledgment)*. But there is [**another / possible**] [**explanation / line of argument**]… *(response)*.
- Some evidence [**might / may / could**] [**suggest / indicate / point to**] that we should… *(acknowledgment)*, but… *(response)*.

# Conclusions

Writing a conclusion uses the same elements as the introduction:

- State your main point near the beginning of the conclusion — but **do not repeat the exact words**.
- Highlight the significance.
- Suggest future research lines.

# Writing a Research Paper

> **Part 3**

## Steps in research

1. Idea
2. Read; then think, analyze, model, create, choose, etc.
3. Build a prototype
4. Test, measure, compare
5. Writing
6. Publication (short paper, poster, abstract…)

## Why write a scientific paper?

- A scientific paper is a written and published report describing **original research results**.
- It improves your own work: it helps you remember, understand, and test your thinking.
- It explains your ideas and makes them accessible to others, advancing knowledge in your field with evidence.
- Two key components: an **explicit claim** about your contribution, and **evidence** to support it.

## The research paper — the final step

> "Research is complete only when the results are shared with the scientific community."
>
> — APA (2010), *Publication Manual of the American Psychological Association* (6th ed.)

**Process of publication:** completion of research → preparation of manuscript → submission → assignment and review → decision (rejection / revision) → resubmission → re-review → acceptance or rejection → publication.

## What makes a good research paper?

Three ingredients: **good science**, **good writing**, and a **good journal**.

### Good science

- Solves a problem for the first time, opens a new field, etc.
- Performs better than peers' work in behavior, efficiency, coverage, usability, …

### Good writing

There is no single best way — it varies from paper to paper. Reading a research paper should be like watching a good movie or reading a good book: you don't notice the sound quality, the grammar, or the formatting; you get immersed in the story. The point: your manuscript should be **flawless** in grammar, spelling, sentence structure, and formatting — easier said than done.

**The narrative flow:**

- Here is a problem.
- It's an interesting problem.
- It's an unsolved problem.
- **Here is my idea.**
- My idea works (details, data).
- Here's how my idea compares to other approaches.

> The reader thinks: *"I wish I knew how to solve that!"* → *"I see how that works. Ingenious!"*

Good writing tells a story the reader follows from start to end. Writing is sequential and **logic is the glue**: sentences hold hands, transitions are smooth, and every step feels inevitable.

**Be careful with:**

- **Content & accuracy** — avoid inconsistencies and ambiguous words.
- **Clarity.**
- **Concision** — use the fewest, shortest words (one word instead of many).
- **Logic & order of presentation.**

Beware of bad writing: if the words don't do justice to your ideas — multiple spelling and syntax mistakes — the reviewer suspects similar sloppiness in the lab.

### Good journal

**Metrics:**

- **Impact Factor** — the average number of times a journal's papers are cited up to two years after publication. *(E.g. articles published in 2003 cited in 2003, divided by articles published in 2003.)*
- **Immediacy Index** — the average number of citations during the year of publication.

**Fit matters more than metrics alone** — also consider readership and the types of papers the journal publishes. Choose the journal **before** writing.

**Strategies to choose a journal:**

- Where do the scientists you cite publish?
- Read the journal's advertising statements and its "scope" paragraph carefully.
- Read the table of contents and examine several articles in candidate journals.

## Organization of a research paper

| Part | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Title** | — |
| **Abstract** | — |
| **Introduction** | What question (problem) was studied |
| **Material & Methods** | How the problem was studied |
| **Results & Discussion** | What the findings are, what they mean, how they compare, limitations |
| **Conclusion** | Recap of the main findings |
| **References** | In the appropriate style |

Our formatting requirements follow the CHI proceedings format:
<https://chi2020.acm.org/authors/chi-proceedings-format/>

## Title, authors and affiliation

### Title

- The first thing readers read — maximum information in the fewest words (less than 12 words / 100 characters).
- A title is a **label**: avoid abbreviations.
- A question can be easier to understand and have more impact.
- Review the title once the writing is done.

### Authorship

Authors should be persons who:

- Generated and can defend at least part of the intellectual content (idea, design, data analysis, interpretation).
- Drafted or reviewed the work for important intellectual content.
- Approved the final version to be published.
- Are willing to concede publicly any errors.

**Order** — most journals assume an order based on contribution:

- The **first author** is primarily responsible for collecting and analyzing data and for writing.
- The **last author**, an established investigator, assumes overall responsibility for the study.
- **Middle authors** are listed by their order of importance to the study.
- All funding sources and institutional/corporate affiliations must be acknowledged.

**Responsibilities** — when submitting, authors confirm that:

- The manuscript is not under consideration elsewhere and will not be submitted elsewhere until a final decision is made.
- The manuscript is a trustworthy, original work without fabrication, fraud, or plagiarism.
- They have made an important scientific contribution and are familiar with the primary data.
- They have read the manuscript and take responsibility for its content, sharing responsibility if it is found faulty or fraudulent.

## Abstract

- Precise, **informative** (not merely descriptive) writing.
- Some numbers, but not in excess.
- Determines whether the paper will be read, and is distributed freely in databases.

**Example:**

> This study addresses to what extent spatial mnemonics can be used to help users memorize or infer a set of text-input chords. Users mentally visualize each character as a 3×3 pixel grid, input as a sequence of three chords using one, two, or three fingers. Experiments show users can apply the strategy after a few minutes of instruction, and some enter text unaided after three hours of practice. Text can be input at a mean rate of 5.9 words per minute (9.9 for the fastest subject). On the downside, the approach has a relatively high error rate of about 10%, as subjects often resort to trial and error when recalling character patterns.
>
> — <https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1133265.1133313>

## Keywords

*(Add the key concepts of the paper.)*

## Introduction

- Two or three paragraphs, fewer than 450 words.
- The opening section of the paper:
  - **First paragraph — context:** motivation and background (common ground).
  - **Second paragraph — problem:** establish explicit reasoning; incomplete knowledge and its consequences; usually a literature review.
  - **Last paragraph — answer:** where the paper is headed; usually finishes with the paper's structure.

## Method

- Tells the reader how the system / algorithm / experiment was designed and carried out — "what you did and how you did it."
- Write it in an entirely straightforward fashion.
- Test: *"Could this research be reproduced?"* — give enough information for an experienced investigator to repeat your work.
- Research-area conventions dictate how the section is divided. Keep it as short as possible and avoid unnecessary detail.
- Typical HCI subsections: **Participants**, **Apparatus**, **Procedure**, **Design**.
- If the hardware/software/algorithm is elaborate, give the details in an earlier section and refer the reader back to it.

## Results and Discussion

Following the method section, the results are given — often combined with discussion (sometimes a separate discussion section follows).

- Present results with statistical tests, charts, and tables, as appropriate.
- Explain the results through discussion.
- Compare your results with prior work on a similar topic, citing as appropriate: is the new technique faster or slower, more or less accurate?

**Structuring the discussion:**

- **First paragraph** — state the major findings; paraphrase the abstract.
- **Middle paragraphs** — base each on a major result; focus on **your** results; never discuss prior work without reference to your own; refer to tables and figures.
- **Last paragraph** — *"In summary…"* (2–3 sentences); *"In conclusion…"* state the biggest message, avoid speculation, and avoid "more work is needed."

## Conclusion & References

*(Recap the main findings; list references in the appropriate style.)*

## Drafting

**First draft:**

- Write as quickly as possible, as if thinking out loud — get everything down.
- Ignore spelling, grammar, and style; skip troublesome words.
- Correct and rewrite only when the **whole** text is on paper.
- Don't split the manuscript among co-authors.

**Successive drafts:**

- All first drafts have too many words — reshape, refine, and prune vigorously.
- Strip every sentence; look for excessive adverbs and adjectives. *Writing improves in proportion to the deletion of unnecessary words.*
- Strengthen transitions for narrative flow.
- After several drafts, ask for a second opinion.

**Final words:**

- Read successful writers and good papers — imitate them.
- Details matter.
- Good writing takes time — start early.
- Write every day, keep improving, and ask for help.
- It is better to write something and edit later than to write nothing — ideas come while writing.
- *The secret of writing is re-writing; the secret of re-writing is rethinking.*
- Writing is a craft, not an art. **Practice, practice, practice!**

# The Introduction in Detail

> **Part 3 — the introduction**

Recall the structure: **two or three paragraphs, fewer than 450 words** — context, problem, answer.

## Example 1

> **Context:** As part of its program of Continuous Quality Improvement ("CQI"), Motodyne Computers plans to redesign the user interface… Motodyne has three years' experience with its current icon set…
> **Problem:** …but it has no data showing which icons are self-explanatory. Lacking such data, we cannot determine which icons to redesign.
> **Response:** This report provides data for eleven icons, showing that five of them are not self-explanatory.

## Example 2

> **Context:** In today's society, would Major John André be hanged [for spying]? It is the only offense that mandates death.
> **Problem:** Recently, however, the Supreme Court has rejected mandatory death sentences in civilian cases, creating an ambiguity in their application to military cases. Will Congress have to revise the Uniform Code of Military Justice?
> **Response:** This article concludes that it will.

## The three steps

**Step 1 — establish common ground.** The opening context establishes a shared understanding about the larger issue. The writer then disrupts this stable context with a problem, saying in effect: *"Reader, you may think you know something, but your knowledge is flawed or incomplete."*

**Step 2 — state your problem.** A research problem has two parts: a *condition* of incomplete knowledge or understanding, and the *consequences* of that condition — a more significant gap in understanding.

**Step 3 — state your response.** State the gist of your solution explicitly, toward the end of the introduction.

## Writing a strong introduction

- **A compelling hook** — one or two sentences that grab attention: a powerful declaration, a fact, an intriguing question, a striking quotation.
- **Important background** — what the reader needs to understand the topic; describe the problem, starting general and finishing specific.
- **A provable, specific statement** — a sentence connecting your topic with what you want to prove.

**Example 3:**

> Did you know that over 2.3 million Americans are currently incarcerated? While it is widely believed that prison should be the punishment for crime, statistics show that 77% of prisoners re-offend once released — suggesting prison is not working to reduce recidivism. This paper aims to demonstrate that prison does not work as a way to prevent crime, and that alternatives such as restorative justice and community sentencing should be considered.

**Example 4:**

> What would happen to humanity if everyone just stopped having babies? Although more endemic in some places than others, the global decline in birth rates has become a major issue since the end of the pandemic. This research shows not only that birth rates are declining all over the world, but also that — unless the threats are addressed — these drastic declines will only get worse.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Lacking a feeling of direction or purpose
- Giving away too much
- Overly long paragraphs
- Excessive *or* insufficient background, literature, and theory
- Including material that belongs in the body or conclusion
- Writing too little or too much
- Using too many quotes

Write the introduction of your research, including a tentative title and the author's name.

- **First paragraph — context:** a compelling hook (a powerful declaration, fact, question, or quotation).
- **Second paragraph — problem:** background needed to understand the topic; problem description; usually a literature review.
- **Last paragraph — answer:** where the paper is headed.

# Writing in English

> **Part 3 — writing in English**

The trend in English sentence length:

| Year | Average length |
|---|---|
| 1600 | 40–60 words |
| 1900 | 21 words |
| 1970 | 17 words |
| Now | 12–17 words |

Scientific writing trends toward **shorter sentences with less punctuation** — short sentences are the norm and don't sound childish. **One sentence, one idea. Simplify!** Several short sentences do the job better than one over-long one.

> The 3 C Principles of technical English writing: **Correct · Clear · Concise.**

## Spelling

Useful online dictionaries:

- <http://www.wordreference.com/>
- <http://www.merriam-webster.com/>
- <http://www.thefreedictionary.com/>

Use spellcheckers — but remember they won't catch mistakes like **from / form** or **trial / trail**.

## Rule 1 — use simple words and effective verbs

- Are you trying to express ideas, or to show people you are a great scholar? The more obscure the word, the greater the chance of using it wrong.
- A Mochou Lake Park sign used "pulchritude" — most native English speakers don't know it. Try **beautiful**.
- **KIS — Keep It Simple.**

Replace weak verb phrases with single effective verbs:

| Phrase | Verb |
|---|---|
| figure out | determine |
| go up to | reach |
| keep up | maintain |
| go down | decrease |
| make up | constitute |
| draw a conclusion | conclude |
| make an analysis | analyze |
| make a consideration | consider |
| perform a simulation | simulate |
| conduct an investigation | investigate |

A paper is more readable when words are used economically. Writing concisely may run contrary to practice in places where authors are paid by the word — but your goal is to **facilitate communication** through concise, lucid, well-organized writing.

## Rule 2 — use simple sentence structure

Few errors are made with simple subject–verb–object sentences. The writing may be less "interesting," but it will most likely be correct and understandable.

| Complicated | Simple |
|---|---|
| While Jane was with Paul she was seen by Dick. | Dick saw Jane with Paul. |
| Computations were conducted on the data. | The data were calculated. |
| It may seem reasonable to suggest that the necrotic effect may possibly be due to toxins. | Necrosis may be caused by toxins. |
| In studies pertaining to identification of phenolic derivatives, drying of the paper gives less satisfactory visualization. | Phenolic derivatives are easier to see if the paper is left wet. |

**Avoid unclear pronouns** (*it, this, that, these, they*). Join ideas with *this/these + noun*:

> "According to a recent survey, 26% of all American adults — down from 38% thirty years ago — now smoke. **This drop** can be partly attributed to the mounting evidence linking smoking and fatal disease, such as cancer."

## Rule 3 — get rid of fluff and fill words

When writing science, be clear and use as few words as possible. If removing a word or phrase doesn't alter the meaning or flow, take it out. **When in doubt, take it out.**

**Seldom-needed phrases:** *of course, as a matter of fact, needless to say, in fact, generally speaking, in other words, as we all know, actually, it is said that, in my opinion.*

**Use one word instead of a phrase:**

| Phrase | Word |
|---|---|
| a majority of | most |
| a number of | many, several, some |
| at a rapid rate | rapidly |
| as a consequence of | because of |
| at this point in time | now, currently |
| based on the fact that | because |
| completely filled | filled |
| despite the fact that | although |
| due to the fact that | because |
| during the course of | during, while |
| fewer in number | fewer |
| for the purpose of examining | to examine |
| has the capability of | can, is able to |
| in all cases | always, invariably |
| in connection with | about, concerning |
| in the event that | if |
| in the near future | soon |

| it is possible that the cause is | the cause may be |
| it is worth pointing out that | note that |

| large amounts of | much |

## Rule 4 — proofread and correct

- It is hard to proof your own work. Proofread one sentence at a time, asking:
  - Is this really what I intended to say?
  - Are modifiers close to what they modify?
  - Should the prepositional phrases at the end be moved?
  - Does the order of sentences present the message best?
  - Are there misspelled words the spellchecker missed, or two words that should be one?
- Delay proofreading so you can take a fresh look. **Read out of order** — from the last sentence back to the first.
- Have a good, critical friend proofread.

## Rule 5 — remove repetition of words

Check for repeated words and phrases:

> "My grandpa went to town. My grandpa bought some eggs. Then my grandpa came home."

> "The house was very big. It was very beautiful. I am very glad to have a house. We are very very lucky to have so much room."

*Very* is a very lazy word — find a word that says what you mean without needing "very."

## Rule 6 — eliminate redundancy of ideas

Don't repeat the same thought across or within sentences — once is enough.

- "My grandmother was an intelligent woman. She was very smart." *(redundant)*
- "The English language is difficult to learn. I have to work hard to learn English." *(redundant)*
- "I will take an interdisciplinary and cross-subject approach to my academic studies." *(redundant)*

# Commonly Confused and Misused Words

**Articles — a/an vs. the**

- **a / an** — used when the noun can exist in more than one form or case: *"**A** new species of Escherichia was identified."*
- **the** — used when no more than one instance exists or is likely to: *"**The** organism responsible for the outbreak was Escherichia coli."*

**because / as / since**

- **as** — temporal, not causal: *"**As** we were completing the paper, new evidence came to light."*
- **because** — causal: *"Five participants could not complete the follow-up **because** they had moved out of the district."*
- **since** — temporal: *"**Since** we completed the study, there have been 3 additional outbreaks."*

**because of / due to**

- **due to** = "attributable to": *"The problem was **due to** mechanical failure."*
- **because of** = "as a result of" / "owing to": *"The problem occurred **because of** mechanical failure."*
- *Tip: substitute "attributable to" and "as a result of" to test which is correct.*

**comparable / similar (and same)**

- **comparable** — lending itself to comparison: *"Because the methods are different, the statistics are not **comparable**."*
- **similar** — indicating likeness: *"The mortality rates in Sweden and Chile are **similar**."*
- *Tip: "Same" means identical — rarely the case when comparing scientific results.*

**compose / comprise / constitute**

- **compose** — to form or make up; the whole *is composed of* its parts: *"Twenty-three provinces **compose** China." / "China **is composed of** 23 provinces."*
- **comprise** — to include, contain, be made up of: *"China **comprises** 23 provinces."*
- **constitute** — same as "compose."

**currently / presently / at present (and now)**

- **currently** (preferred) and **at present** mean "now": *"There are **currently** no studies on Han Chinese populations."*
- **presently** means "soon" / "shortly": *"**Presently**, we will go to the Forbidden City."*
- *Tip: "Now" is often used when "currently" or "at present" is better.*

**demonstrate / exhibit / reveal / show**

- **demonstrate** — only for *deliberate action* to illustrate a procedure: *"The technician **demonstrated** how to operate the pH meter."*
- **exhibit** — deliberate action to make visible (not passive carrying): *"He **exhibited** the mineral specimens at the congress."*
- **reveal** — to make visible what was hidden (not a synonym for "report"): *"The X-ray **revealed** a tumor."*
- *Tip: non-living things cannot demonstrate anything — avoid "The data demonstrated that…"*

**significant vs. important / great / major / valuable**

- **significant** — reserve for the statistical sense, reaching a numeric threshold: *"The mean blood pressure was **significantly** lowered, with a P value of 0.05."*
- Use the other words when statistical significance is **not** intended: *"The results will be of **great** value to nanotechnology researchers."*

**use / employ / utilize**

- **use** — adequate in most cases: *"An autorefractor was **used** to measure refractive error."*
- **employ** — to put a person to work, or an object to use: *"All subjects had been **employed** at the company since 1997 or earlier."*
- *Tip: there is no reason to use "utilize" in scientific writing.*

**affect / effect**

- **affect** — verb, "to influence": *"Chlorine **affects** the taste of water."*
- **effect** — usually a noun, "result": *"Chlorine has an **effect** on the taste of water."* As a verb it means "to bring about": *"Combining chlorine and sodium **effects** a chemical change in which salt is formed."*

**anymore / any more**

- **anymore** — adverb meaning "now," used only at the end of a sentence: *"Their firm is not practicing architecture **anymore**."*
- **any more** — emphasizes something extra: *"I don't want **any more** coffee."*

**a lot / alot · all ready / already**

- **a lot** — "We've done **a lot** of projects like this." (*alot* is **not** a word.)
- **all ready** — "all set": *"We are **all ready** to leave for the cruise."*
- **already** — something has taken place: *"The boat **already** left the dock."*

**all together / altogether · farther / further**

- **all together** — "everyone at the same time" / "in a group": *"We are going out to dinner **all together**."*
- **altogether** — "thoroughly" / "completely": *"That's an **altogether** different problem."*
- **farther** — physical distance: *"New York is **farther** from Maine than Boston."*
- **further** — "more" / "to a greater extent": *"We are not going to discuss this any **further**."*

**assure / ensure / insure**

- **assure** — "to give confidence to": *"I **assure** you that all precautions have been taken."*
- **ensure** — "to make sure / guarantee": *"The bullet-proof windows **ensure** the safety of the occupants."*
- **insure** — to procure insurance: *"It makes sense to **insure** your home and car with the same company."*

**its / it's · percent / percentage**

- **its** — singular possessive pronoun: *"A construction company usually leaves **its** major equipment on site."*
- **it's** — contraction of "it is": *"**It's** normal for a company to leave its equipment on site."*
- **percent** — used with a number: *"About fifty **percent** of sales are lost."*
- **percentage** — used when there is no number: *"A **percentage** of the proceeds will go to charity."*

**regardless / irregardless · than / then**

- **regardless** — *"**irregardless**," like "ain't," is not standard English.*
- **than** — conjunction used in comparisons: *"One firm was more qualified **than** the other."*
- **then** — adverb showing time: *"First we survey the site; **then** we cut down the trees."*

**their / there / they're**

- **their** — plural possessive: *"Companies usually leave **their** large equipment on the job site."*
- **there** — shows place, or begins a sentence: *"Put the plans over **there**." / "**There** are several approaches to the problem."*
- **they're** — "they are": *"**They're** one of three firms competing for the assignment."*

**to / too / two**

- **to** — forms the infinitive: *"We hope **to** design this project."*
- **too** — "also" or "to an excessive degree": *"We hope to join the team, **too**." / "He put **too** much salt on his hamburger."*
- **two** — the number 2.

**your / you're · not only … but also**

- **your** — possessive pronoun: *"**Your** firm was selected."*
- **you're** — "you are": *"**You're** one of the finalists."*
- **not only … but also** — if you use one, use the other: *"**Not only** will we build your building, **but** we will **also** guarantee it meets your goals."*

# Master's Thesis Template — Formatting Rules (MUSI)

> Template for the Master's Thesis for the Master's Degree in Intelligent Systems (MUSI), based on **IEEEtran** and adapted by Ramon Mas, Sebastià Massanet, and Pedro Bibiloni. You can download the source code or duplicate the project for your own use.

## Motivation

These rules are **mandatory** for all students completing the master's thesis. Failure to comply may result in the thesis not being accepted.

## Fundamental rules

### Body, font, and line styles

The body style, font, and line style cannot be modified:

- The body is divided into **two columns**.
- The text is in **Times font, 10 pt**.
- The text must be **justified** on both margins.
- On the **last page**, the columns must be balanced.
- A new paragraph is indicated by **indenting the first word**.

### Figures and tables

Figures and tables may be placed within a column or across the full body width. In both cases the object is centered, and the two environments are numbered independently.

- **Figure captions** begin with *Figure n:*, use regular text at 10 pt, and are placed **below** the figure.
- **Table captions** are placed **above** the table.

Placement:

- An object **within a column** can go anywhere in that column.
- An object **spanning both columns** can only be placed at the **top or bottom** of the page.

*Example figures:* a figure within a column (full column width), and a figure within the body (full body width, e.g. a PhD Comics source image).

### Length

## Bibliography

- Use the **`abbrv`** style.
